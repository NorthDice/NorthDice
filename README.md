  <h2>💼 What I Do</h2>
  <p>
    I currently work as a <strong>Go & AI Backend Engineer</strong> at <strong><a href="https://monobank.ua/en/">Monobank</a></strong> — Ukraine's largest neobank, serving millions of users with a fully app-based banking experience and one of the highest-rated financial apps in the country. It's a place where engineering standards are high and the systems you build actually matter at scale.
  </p>

  <h3>⚙️ Backend Engineering</h3>
  <p>
    I've always been drawn to what happens behind the scenes. While others focus on what users see, I find myself more excited about what they don't — the systems running underneath, the data pipelines, the architecture decisions that make everything hold together under load. There's something deeply satisfying about working backstage: understanding how a system truly works from the inside, tracing a request through layers of code, and knowing exactly why it's fast or why it's slow. Go fits this mindset perfectly — it doesn't hide complexity behind magic, it exposes it, and I love that.
  </p>

  <h3>🤖 AI Systems</h3>
  <p>
    What excites me most about working with AI isn't the hype — it's the engineering challenge underneath. But there's another side to it I didn't expect: sometimes, when reviewing AI-generated code, I find myself in the role of a senior engineer. I question its decisions, push back on its assumptions, and search for better patterns and practices. It's become one of the best ways I've found to sharpen my own thinking — treating the model not as an oracle, but as a junior dev whose work deserves real scrutiny.
  </p>

  <h3>📊 Observability</h3>
  <p>
    I find something uniquely mine in working with the terminal. There's a certain clarity to it — no abstractions, no UI layer, just you and the system communicating directly. Metrics, logs, dashboards — these are how I make sense of what's really happening inside a running service. Taking something opaque and making it visible, building a Grafana panel that finally explains a weird latency spike — that kind of work genuinely satisfies me. If a system can't be observed, it can't be trusted.
  </p>
</div>

<div>
  <h2>💡 Tech Philosophy</h2>
  <ul>
    <li>📝 <strong>Clean, maintainable code</strong> that others can understand and extend</li>
    <li>🏗️ <strong>Scalable architecture</strong> designed for real production load</li>
    <li>📊 <strong>Observability first</strong> — if you cannot measure it, you cannot improve it</li>
    <li>🔄 <strong>Continuous learning</strong> in the fast-evolving AI landscape</li>
  </ul>
  <p>
    AI-assisted development is the future — and I embrace it. Tools like LLMs can genuinely accelerate your workflow, help you explore ideas faster, and surface patterns you might have missed. But I believe you should never just accept what the model gives you. Review it critically, question its assumptions, and always stay one step ahead. The engineer who knows <em>why</em> the code works will always outperform the one who just copies it.
  </p>
  <p>
    I also care deeply about the human side of software. At the end of the day, everything we build is used by people — and that responsibility matters. Good UX isn't just a designer's job; it starts with how you think about your system, your APIs, your error messages. I try to keep that perspective close, even when working deep in the backend.
  </p>
